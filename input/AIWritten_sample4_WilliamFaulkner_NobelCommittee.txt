Born William Cuthbert Falkner on September 25, 1897, in New Albany, Mississippi, he grew up in nearby Oxford, a place that shaped both his imagination and the fictional landscapes of his later work. His family’s history, marked by the fading grandeur of the Old South, provided a deep reservoir of stories and tensions that would later animate his novels. 

Faulkner’s early years were marked by an uneven relationship with formal education. He left high school without graduating and pursued what he later described as “undirected reading,” absorbing literature on his own terms. In 1918 he attempted to join the U.S. Army but was rejected for his size; instead, he enlisted in the Canadian Royal Air Force, though he never saw combat. After the war, he briefly attended the University of Mississippi before drifting through a series of jobs in New York, New Orleans, and back home in Mississippi. These experiences, combined with his voracious reading, helped shape his emerging literary voice. 

His first novels in the mid‑1920s attracted limited attention, but everything changed with The Sound and the Fury (1929), a daring experiment in narrative structure and stream‑of‑consciousness technique. This novel, followed by As I Lay Dying (1930) and Light in August (1932), established Faulkner as a central figure in modernist literature. These works were set in Yoknapatawpha County, his richly imagined fictional world modeled on Lafayette County, Mississippi. Through this setting, he explored the decline of the Old South, the weight of history, racial tensions, and the psychological depths of his characters. 

Faulkner’s writing was not limited to novels. He also worked in Hollywood as a screenwriter, contributing to films such as To Have and Have Not and The Big Sleep. Though he found the work financially useful, he regarded it as secondary to his fiction. His literary reputation grew steadily, especially after the publication of The Portable Faulkner in 1946, which helped introduce his work to a broader audience. In 1949 he received the Nobel Prize in Literature for what the committee called his “powerful and unique contribution to the modern American novel.” He later won two Pulitzer Prizes for A Fable (1954) and The Reivers (1962). 

In his later years, Faulkner served as writer‑in‑residence at the University of Virginia, continuing to write and lecture while maintaining his home in Oxford. He died on July 6, 1962, in Byhalia, Mississippi, leaving behind a body of work that reshaped American fiction. His legacy endures in the complexity of his characters, the innovation of his narrative techniques, and the haunting power of his vision of the American South.
